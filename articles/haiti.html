all about da missing benjamins: revisiting haiti’s red cross scandal

edit: this article was originally written as an academic research paper and has since been reworked to fit this platform.

in 2010, haiti suffered from a devastating earthquake that left many of its people without homes, hope, and even their loved ones. in response, the american red cross launched a massive relief effort, where a groundbreaking total of $500 million was reached in donations. but with half a billion raised, why the hell does haiti have so little to show for it? we need to figure this out.

[usgs, anthony crone, 2010]

in the eighteenth century, haiti was considered the wealthiest colony in the americas and, quite frankly, the world. the country produced and supplied half of the world with coffee, indigo, cotton, and sugar. i'm sure this makes it very hard to grasp the concept of a once-wealthy colony becoming the poorest country in the western hemisphere to date. over the years, haiti has been represented in the media as a destitute and broken country. it is often cursed with the image of being dark and resourceless, with nothing but foreign debt, corruption, and underdevelopment to show for it.

haiti has been placed in such an unfortunate space, and it’s been indoctrinated in our minds due to our daily media consumption. however, I don’t think it’s fair to leave their impoverished circumstances at face value without context. it’s extremely important to me that i explain why and how haiti has ended up in such a manner in the first place.

exploitation, political instability, and a september 2004 flood played an incredible part in the country's demise; however, it was a january 12th, 2010, earthquake that, for lack of a better term, took the haitian community’s world by storm and caused severe devastation. this 7.0 earthquake in the country's epicenter of port-au-prince caused the people of the land to not only lose their homes, schools, and businesses but also their lives. according to un News, 220,000 people were reportedly killed during this natural disaster, and about 1.5 million became homeless due to the 35-second-long tremor.
